## 1\. Lumen Technologies Inc. (LUMN)

###### *Current Price: \~$6.40 | Price Target: $7.43 (16% upside) | Rating: Overweight*

Article Title: "The Internet Runs on Lumen's Pipes, Somebody Tell the Market"

The debt crisis is behind Lumen, the hyperscaler deals are signed, and the infrastructure story is genuinely compelling at a moment when the world is discovering that AI runs on fiber. At current levels the stock reflects more fear than conviction, which is either an opportunity or a trap depending on whether management keeps delivering quarters that look less like a wireline company and more like one that built the highway system before anyone owned a car. Moby's target by early 2027 assumes the unglamorous work continues: more fiber, more recovered costs, more deferred revenue turning active one quarter at a time. Boring, if it works.

Why Lumen Is Winning

Lumen is not a telecom in any traditional sense. It is the plumbing of the internet, maintaining 400,000 route miles of fiber across 60-plus countries with a customer base anchored in the enterprises and governments that cannot function without it. The company spent three years in what amounted to a slow-motion restructuring after aggressive acquisitions left it drowning in debt and legacy copper obligations. The turnaround was not glamorous. It involved years of painful debt restructuring, asset sales, and brutal guidance cuts. However, what remains after all that pain is a company with real assets, a cleaner balance sheet, and something that looks increasingly like a compelling thesis for the AI infrastructure era.

The core of the current story is the Private Connectivity Fabric program. Through deals with Microsoft, Meta, and Google collectively worth roughly $13 billion, Lumen is selling dedicated fiber capacity to hyperscalers who need to move massive amounts of data between data centers without relying on congested public internet infrastructure. These are not ordinary bandwidth contracts. They are structured PCF agreements covering specific, dedicated routes with long-term commitments, effectively turning Lumen's fiber network into reserved private highways for AI workloads.

Financials

2025 revenue came in at $12.4 billion, down from $13.1 billion in 2024, which sounds bad until you look at what's driving it. Every dollar migrating from legacy copper to high-margin fiber improves the company's long-term earnings power even as it temporarily shrinks the top line. Q4 2025 non-GAAP EPS of $0.23 against an expected loss of $0.27 is the kind of beat that gets people's attention.

The more significant event was February 2, 2026, when Lumen completed the $5.75 billion sale of its mass-market consumer fiber business to AT\&T. Total debt fell below $13 billion, the credit profile improved materially, and the company locked in roughly $500 million in annual interest savings. Add the $13 billion in secured PCF contracts from Microsoft, Meta, and Google, and management's guidance of $1.2 to $1.4 billion in free cash flow for 2026 starts to look less like optimism and more like arithmetic. The stock's current multiple still prices in a fair amount of legacy-era skepticism, which is either a problem or an opportunity.

Opportunities

Global data consumption is estimated to double roughly every 18 months over the next decade. The shift toward 8K streaming, real-time AI inference, and industrial IoT isn't slowing down, and the physical infrastructure required to support it has long lead times and meaningful barriers to entry. Fiber scarcity has gone from a hypothetical risk to an as-of-yet unscheduled future event.

Lumen's position inside that is straightforward: 17 million intercity fiber miles deployed as of year-end 2025, on track for 47 million by the end of 2028, and a NaaS customer base that doubled to more than 2,000 businesses in early 2026\. When hyperscalers and global corporations start buying network architecture rather than commodity bandwidth, the competitive conversation changes considerably. Lumen appears to be approaching that inflection, which is either the thesis or the hope, depending on how charitable you're feeling about telecom management teams on any given morning.

Stock Outlook

The $7.43 consensus target is not a smooth escalator ride from here. The $10.50 bull case requires AI revenue inflection to genuinely outpace legacy runoff, a question that has no clean answer yet. CEO Kate Johnson gave the clearest available signal of her own conviction in February 2026, purchasing $500,000 worth of shares at $6.35 on the open market. Executives do that when they believe the thesis or want you to believe they believe it, and in this case the fundamentals support giving her the benefit of the doubt. The market's continued caution is, for long-duration investors, the entire point of being here.

Risks

The core risk is timing. The $13 billion in PCF deals are long-term contracts, and long-term contracts have a habit of appearing in guidance well before they appear in the income statement. Full revenue inflection probably doesn't arrive until 2028, which means the legacy copper business needs to decline politely and on schedule rather than accelerate its own obsolescence ahead of plan. If copper erodes faster than the fiber pipeline matures, the cash flow buffer funding the growth segment gets thinner in exactly the wrong window. AT\&T and Verizon are not standing still, and both carry balance sheets that make Lumen's capital expenditure flexibility look constrained by comparison.

Conclusion

The debt crisis is behind it, the hyperscaler deals are signed, and the infrastructure story is genuinely compelling at a moment when the world is discovering that AI runs on fiber. ## 2\. Cognizant Technology Solutions (CTSH)

###### *Current Price: $61.05 | Price Target: $93 (50% upside) | Rating: Overweight | Upcoming Earnings: April 29*

Article Title: "Cognizant Is the Unloved Compounder That's Making the AI Hype Machine Look Silly"

Wall Street has a well-documented allergy to businesses that quietly execute while everyone else is busy having a moment, which goes a long way toward explaining why Cognizant Technology Solutions trades where it does. The company built a $30 billion operation guiding legacy enterprises through digital transformation, the kind of engagement that never trends on Fintwit but does tend to renew for three to five years at nine figures. While the valley spent the last decade rewarding narrative over numbers, Cognizant was compounding in the background like a character you forget about in act one who turns out to own everything by the finale. Moby sees $93 by Q4 2026, and the only thing required to get there is for the market to update its priors.

Why Cognizant Is Winning

Cognizant began its life in 1994 as an in-house technology unit for Dun & Bradstreet, a pedigree that baked data discipline into its DNA from the first line of code. It survived the 2008 crash by becoming essential to the financial services and healthcare sectors, two industries that cannot stop spending on technology even when the world is ending. Today, as global firms spend billions on AI ethics and data privacy, CTSH has emerged as a primary beneficiary of what you might call "Force for Good" spending, a generous way of saying enterprises are terrified and writing large checks to whoever seems most prepared.

Recent history shows a company that decided to stop being polite about its ambitions. Under current leadership, Cognizant signed 28 deals valued at over $100 million each in 2025 alone, which is less a sign of sales prowess than a reflection of a corporate world that got tired of managing fifty different boutique consultants and wants one throat to choke. By acquiring firms like Belcan and Thirdera, it expanded into high-end engineering R\&D and the ServiceNow ecosystem, moving up the food chain from the people who fix the computers to the architects who design the digital strategy. The org chart equivalent of getting promoted out of the mailroom and into the boardroom, except the mailroom was already billing $20 billion a year.

The winning edge in the current cycle comes from how Cognizant handles the AI complexity problem. Rather than treating AI as a buzzword and waiting for applause, it fills the massive skill gap for a world terrified of being left behind, and its 2026 Neuro AI platform has seen rapid adoption among Fortune 500 firms looking to automate back-office operations and reduce human error in healthcare and finance. Over 30% of its developer effort is now AI-assisted, which matters enormously in a services business where productivity is the ultimate competitive moat. If Cognizant can deliver a transformation project 20% faster than its competitors because its engineers are effectively cyborgs, it can win on price without sacrificing its own margins, a combination that most of its competitors are still trying to figure out how to PowerPoint their way around.

Financials

In FY2025, Cognizant reported $21.1 billion in revenue, representing 7% growth in an environment where most IT discretionary budgets were treated like New Year's resolutions: made in January, quietly abandoned by March. Non-GAAP EPS jumped 11% to $5.28, the product of a six-quarter streak of organic growth that the market has somehow decided doesn't count. The balance sheet reads like a company that knows it has options, with debt-to-capital in the mid-single digits, a current ratio above 2, and enough free cash to pay a $1.32 annual dividend while still going shopping. Cognizant can afford to be aggressive right now precisely because its peers are being defensive.

For FY2026, management is guiding for revenue between $22.14 billion and $22.66 billion, with operating margins landing between 15.9% and 16.1%. The expansion is slight, but that's somewhat the point. A company quietly extracting more profit from every dollar of revenue doesn't need to announce it.

Opportunities

Here is a useful way to think about what Cognizant actually sells. Imagine a major bank or hospital system has spent 30 years building its technology infrastructure on a stack that was never designed for the internet, let alone AI. The code works, technically, the same way a 1994 Toyota Camry technically gets you to the grocery store. What these organizations need isn't a new strategy deck; they need someone to gut the plumbing while the building stays open. That is Cognizant's whole business, and it is genuinely difficult work, which is why they keep winning large, multi-year contracts to do it, and why enterprises simplifying their vendor lists keep defaulting to Cognizant as the safe pair of hands.

AI then opens a second, rather elegant opportunity on the internal side. Cognizant has been shifting toward fixed-bid and transaction-based contracts, which means that when its own AI tools cut delivery time in half, the savings don't flow back to the client. They flow to Cognizant. The company gets to keep the efficiency premium. Wall Street has been strangely slow to price this in, possibly because "IT services firm using AI internally to protect its own margins" doesn't make for a compelling conference presentation.

Stock Outlook

Cognizant currently trades at a forward P/E significantly below pure-play AI software firms, a valuation that implies the market sees it as a structurally declining business rather than what it actually is: a profitable, growing technology company with a long runway of genuinely necessary work ahead of it. That gap between perception and reality is Moby's primary justification for a $93 price target by Q4 2026\.

The bull case requires no particularly creative assumptions. If Cognizant continues posting 4% to 7% revenue growth while sustaining 16% margins, a re-rating toward 16x or 17x on projected 2026 earnings gets you to $93 with math that almost feels embarrassingly clean. The market's chronic cynicism about the consulting sector has to loosen at some point. It tends to.

Risks

The most immediate risk is visa policy. Cognizant runs on a global talent base, and U.S. immigration friction doesn't just create HR headaches, it creates real cost pressure and delivery complications that no amount of internal AI deployment can fully offset in the short term. The second risk is a more structural question: what happens to IT services demand if AI becomes so good that enterprises genuinely need less consulting work done? This is the bear case that Wall Street keeps whispering about. The counterargument is that complexity creates demand for expertise, and AI makes systems more complex, not less.

Conclusion

Cognizant Technology Solutions is the kind of stock that doesn't get discussed at dinner parties. Analysts who follow it tend to use words like "steady" and "consistent," which in modern markets might as well be a restraining order. But steady and consistent, compounded at 10-12% annually for five years while everyone else chases the next large-cap AI announcement, is actually how wealth gets built.

The company has a balance sheet that can weather a downturn, a business model that benefits from AI without needing to market itself as an AI company, and a valuation that prices in roughly zero credit for either of those things. If management hits its 2026 targets, the current share price will look like the market was briefly, inexplicably asleep at the wheel. Our $93 target by Q4 2026 reflects the straightforward premise that markets tend to wake up eventually.

3\. Cameco Corporation (CCJ)  
Current Price: $110.31 | Price Target: $142 (22% upside) | Rating: Overweight | Upcoming Earnings: May 7  
Article Title: "Cameco Corporation is Our Favorite Nuclear Survivalist"

Cameco Corporation is the blue-chip survivalist of the nuclear age. While the rest of the uranium trade spent the last decade wandering the desert, Cameco was busy vertically integrating itself into the very plumbing of the global grid. This is a mining company, a fuel architect, and a reactor mechanic wrapped inside one massive market cap. The market has finally realized that if you want the lights to stay on in a carbon-free future, you eventually have to call Saskatoon. Moby's price target of $142 by Q1 2027 reflects a world where nuclear is no longer a "maybe" and Cameco is the only one with the keys to the kingdom.

Why Cameco Is Winning

Cameco wins because it refused to play the commodity lottery. Most uranium miners are essentially geological wildcatters, living and dying by the spot price of ore. Cameco decided to own the entire cul-de-sac. This dominance is the result of a decades-long grind that began when "nuclear" was a four-letter word in most boardrooms.

Born in 1988 from a merger of government entities, the Saskatchewan Mining Development Corporation and Eldorado Nuclear, Cameco unfurled its banner just as the Chernobyl disaster cratered global faith in the sector. While everyone else was making a frantic retreat, Cameco's first move was to double down on the Athabasca Basin. They spent the 1990s aggressively acquiring high-yield, low-risk deposits like the McArthur River project when uranium was at its most unfashionable. Discovered in 1988 and opened in 1999, McArthur River became the world's largest high-grade uranium mine, giving Cameco a cost-per-pound advantage that few could touch.

By the time the world hit the 2011 Fukushima-Daiichi accident, which sent uranium prices into a decade-long collapse, Cameco proved its discipline. Unlike competitors who tried to "produce their way out" of the crisis, Cameco made the deliberate choice to shutter flagship operations, including McArthur River in 2018, to remove millions of pounds of supply from a flooded market. This maneuver preserved their best assets in the ground while they waited for the "Net Zero" narrative and rising data center power demand to catch up.

The real masterstroke was the pivot into fuel services and the 2023 equity stake in Westinghouse, which turned Cameco from a simple digger of dirt into a high-moat infrastructure partner. By controlling the world's tier-one assets, specifically the Cigar Lake and McArthur River mines and the Key Lake mill, the company secured the high ground in terms of cost and scale. This vertical integration means they provide the outage maintenance, engineering support, and instrumentation that keep the global reactor fleet breathing, a service economy that utilities must pay for regardless of the spot price.

In a world where energy security has gone from a talking point to a constraint, Cameco sits as the strategic architect of the entire nuclear lifecycle. They aren't winning because uranium got expensive; they are winning because they spent 30 years building a fortress that makes them the indispensable bartender of the global energy build-out.

Financials

Cameco's spreadsheet looks like a business that finally stopped apologizing for its existence. Revenue growth has been steady, but the real story is in the margins. Gross margin expanded from single digits in 2020 to more than 25% recently. Net margins followed suit, proving that when operating conditions cooperate, Cameco's bottom line moves faster than its revenue.

The balance sheet is a definitional fortress. With manageable debt-to-capital and strong interest coverage, Cameco has the luxury of being the "adult supervision" in the room. It remains the only dividend payer in its immediate uranium peer set. While the yield is modest, it signals a level of financial maturity its competitors simply cannot fake. Cash flow is being directed toward integration and asset maintenance, ensuring the machine stays greased for the next decade of demand.

Opportunities

The upside is a math problem involving scarcity. Uranium supply is tight, permitting for new mines takes a literal decade, and the secondary supply that used to keep prices suppressed has largely evaporated. Cameco's move into Global Laser Enrichment and its deepening ties to government agencies through Westinghouse position it to capture the "re-industrialization" trade.

As the world moves toward Small Modular Reactors and life extensions for existing plants, Cameco's service-heavy model becomes stickier. This is the "razor and blade" strategy applied to nuclear physics. Selling the fuel is great, but being the only ones who can fix the reactor is better. If the market starts valuing Cameco less like a miner and more like a high-tech infrastructure platform with recurring service value, the current multiples might actually look conservative.

Stock Outlook

The stock lives in a neighborhood of high expectations. It is priced for a standing ovation, not just a polite clap. The valuation is the central tension. It sits above historical norms, reflecting a market that has fully capitalized the bullish nuclear story.

However, institutional confidence remains high. Analysts are following the price action, with targets reflecting growing confidence in the business. Moby's $142 price target by Q1 2027 assumes that earnings momentum, which saw significant upward revisions recently, continues to justify the premium. This is a momentum play backed by real physics and even realer margins.

Risks

Uranium never runs without friction... it's literally uranium. Operational concentration is the primary bogeyman. If something goes sideways at core mines or the Key Lake mill, the narrative takes a hit. The company is also a "priced for perfection" story. When you trade at elevated multiples, even a strong earnings beat is treated as a baseline requirement, and any stumble in the contracting cadence could invite a correction.

Regulatory and policy shifts remain the wildcards. While the current zeitgeist is pro-nuclear, a single safety event or a sudden pivot in energy subsidies could cool the room quickly. There is also "distribution" in the technicals, specifically more volume moving on down days recently, which suggests that while the trend is strong, the market is starting to look over its shoulder.

Conclusion

Cameco Corporation is the definitive way to play the return of physical reality. It generates cash from tier-one assets, integrates itself into the lifecycle of global energy, and sits on a balance sheet that can survive a downturn. The stock needs execution and the continued realization that "clean energy" is a fantasy without the baseload power Cameco provides.

The market remains respectful but cautious. Moby's $142 price target by Q1 2027 predicts a world where Cameco continues to do the unglamorous, essential work of fueling the planet. It is a materials company with a high-value service platform, quietly compounding while louder, flashier energy stories burn out. When the easy money leaves the room, Cameco will still be there, shipping ore and keeping the lights on.

4\. Quanta Services Inc. (PWR)  
Current Price: $574.02 | Price Target: $640 (12% upside) | Rating: Overweight | Upcoming Earnings: May 7  
Article Title: "The Company the Energy Transition Can't Live Without"

Quanta Services is the company that gets called when the world finally stops debating the energy transition and starts digging holes. While most of the market spent the last decade chasing software margins and obsessing over TAM slides, Quanta quietly cornered the market on the physical infrastructure required to keep the lights on and the data centers humming. With a record $44 billion backlog and a presence in everything from 5G expansion to renewable grid modernization, it has become the essential lever for a world that is rapidly electrifying. Our price target of $640 by Q4 2026 reflects a business that has moved past being a mere contractor and into something closer to a critical infrastructure platform.

Why Quanta Is Winning

To understand why Quanta is winning today, you have to go back to the fragmented mess it decided to fix. In the late 1990s, the electrical contracting industry was a patchwork of small regional players who could barely handle a local storm, let alone a national grid overhaul. Quanta recognized that as utilities faced deregulation and aging assets, they would stop trying to manage their own expensive fleets and outsource the headaches to a single, sophisticated partner. We are moving from a Carbon Economy to a Copper Economy, and Quanta is the primary beneficiary of the $2 trillion required to modernize the US grid by 2035\.

It built its lead the hard way, by owning the labor and expertise in environments where failure is not an option. Its self-perform model, where it handles 80% of work in-house, protects it from the supply chain and labor shortages that plague competitors. This is not a company farming out the heavy lifting to an army of subcontractors. It owns the trucks, the specialized tools, and the highly trained humans who know how to hang a high-voltage wire from a helicopter without ending up on the evening news.

The transformative moment arrived in late 2021 when Quanta completed the $2.7 billion acquisition of Blattner Holding Company, at the time the white whale of utility-scale renewable energy infrastructure, with more than 49 gigawatts of wind capacity and 12 gigawatts of solar generation installed across North America. Blattner brought a century-old legacy of engineering excellence and an existing management team, allowing Quanta to offer a full-cycle solution covering everything from front-end engineering to project management and energy storage integration.

The strategic logic was elegant because it solved the single biggest bottleneck in the energy transition: the gap between building a wind farm and actually connecting it to the people who need it. Pairing Blattner's renewable expertise with Quanta's legacy in electric transmission and substations produced a comprehensive solution that competitors simply cannot replicate. Quanta no longer waits for someone else to build the generation before it can build the transmission. Now it builds both, capturing more of the value chain and cementing its status as the architect of the great electrification.

The company's DNA is rooted in high-stakes utility work, but it has scaled horizontally with clinical precision. One segment's engineering discipline compounds across the others, as the same technology used for high-voltage transmission informs its renewable energy and underground infrastructure projects. This diversification is its greatest hedge. When one area of infrastructure spending wobbles, the others carry the weight, allowing it to maintain operating margins north of 25% that would make a traditional builder blush.

Financials

Quanta's financials look like a business that finally stopped apologizing for its capital-intensive nature and started charging for its scarcity. Over the last five years, it has delivered a compound annual growth rate of roughly 17%. In Q4 2025, it put up $7.84 billion in revenue, up nearly 20% year over year, with adjusted EPS of $3.16 handily beating consensus. Full-year 2025 revenue reached $28.5 billion.

The balance sheet is built to sit comfortably through the lumpy cycles of government and utility spending. Quanta reported record free cash flow of $1.7 billion for 2025 and is guiding for even stronger performance in 2026, deploying that cash across eight acquisitions in the year, including a $1.7 billion Q4 splash for Tri-City, Wilson, and Billings Flying Service to bolster its high-voltage and inside electrical capabilities. Even after deploying billions for acquisitions, it maintained a leverage ratio below 2x. It has achieved record revenues in eight of the last nine years and record adjusted diluted earnings per share for nine consecutive years, which is the kind of streak that makes compounders look easy in retrospect and infuriating in real time.

Opportunities

The biggest opportunity for Quanta is less a new product than a math problem. The global push for electrification, driven by everything from the rise of EVs to the massive power demands of AI data centers, requires a grid overhaul that currently does not exist. Data center related work now represents roughly 10% of its business and is the fastest growing portion of its $44 billion backlog.

Management is doubling down by planning a $500 million to $700 million investment in power transformer manufacturing facilities. This is about securing its own supply chain in a world where transformers have years-long lead times. By owning the equipment and the labor, Quanta captures more of the value chain and transforms itself from a contractor into something closer to the toll booth on the highway to electrification. Federal policy tailwinds act as a permanent bid under its services while the shift to renewable energy generates a constant stream of high-margin integration work.

There is also the untapped potential of international expansion. While Quanta dominates North America, it has already established footprints in Canada and Australia, and its integrated solution-based model is a repeatable playbook for other aging global grids. The convergence of the utility, power generation, and large load industries is creating a multi-decade infrastructure transformation, and Quanta sits exactly at the center of that vortex.

Stock Outlook

The stock sits in that awkward but profitable space where it is not cheap enough to be a bargain, yet not expensive enough to scare off anyone who understands the plumbing of the global economy. The market is finally beginning to treat Quanta like a durable platform rather than a cyclical contractor, and that reclassification tends to be a one-way door.

A move toward $640 by Q4 2026 feels earned. Multiple major brokers have recently lifted their targets into that same neighborhood, which suggests this is less a contrarian call than a consensus forming in real time. The market is applying a higher multiple to a business that consistently proves it can handle the complexity of the energy transition, and the smart money has largely already made up its mind, with institutional ownership sitting at 97%. The technical chart reinforces the bullish case, with shares maintaining a strong upward trend above both the 50-day and 200-day moving averages. Momentum indicators suggest the stock may be momentarily stretched, but the underlying fundamentals of a $44 billion backlog provide a high-voltage floor for any minor pullbacks.

Risks

Quanta still lives in the real world of dirt, permits, and regulation. Manufacturing delays or supply chain hiccups in critical components can move project timelines, and any operational stumble on a major contract shows up quickly in the numbers. There is also the risk of backlog addiction, where the market assumes a large pipeline of work automatically translates to perfect execution. Integrating eight acquisitions in a single year brings its own challenges, including the possibility of overpaying or struggling to realize synergies across utility, broadband, and data center projects.

The tight labor market for skilled electrical workers remains a constant hurdle, though its self-perform model gives it a meaningful structural advantage over competitors relying on more fragile subcontractor networks. Any regulatory shifts slowing utility cost recovery could impact the pace of grid investments, though the multi-year nature of federal funding acts as a durable cushion.

Conclusion

Quanta Services is the definition of a quiet compounder. It has moved beyond simple construction into an infrastructure and energy ecosystem that the modern world cannot function without. The growth is steady, the margins are real, and the balance sheet is built for the long haul. Quanta does not need to chase trends. It just needs to wait for the market to realize that you cannot have a digital future without the physical wires it provides, and a $44 billion backlog suggests the room is already getting very crowded.

What's particularly impressive is how Amazon is managing all this growth while also cutting internal fat. The company has been streamlining operations, removing layers of management, and redistributing decision-making to move faster and stay nimble. It's a back-to-basics approach, except the basics now include global logistics, enterprise AI, and next-day delivery in the middle of nowhere.

In short, Amazon isn't just surviving in a complex economic environment. It's thriving. And more importantly, it's evolving from retail titan to AI infrastructure heavyweight. Our updated price target of $225 by Q1 2026 reflects both respect for Amazon's long-term machine and realism about the road ahead. A 22% upside from here is still compelling, but it no longer feels automatic. Investors now need to weigh real short-term friction like rising tariffs, valuation compression, and a consumer who might finally be tightening the belt, against Amazon's structural advantages.

Even with all that, this is still the company that controls the rails of global e-commerce, the backbone of cloud computing, and one of the most efficient ad platforms on Earth. Those aren't just moats. They're fortresses. If AWS keeps growing above 15%, if AI infrastructure starts printing revenue, and if retail continues to lean into higher-margin segments, Amazon's multiple could re-rate fast. But in this phase of the story, conviction isn't enough. Execution is the key.

Risks

\- Tariffs could shave $7-8 billion off EBIT by 2026 as Amazon absorbs costs on China-sourced goods. Passing it to consumers may backfire on pricing.  
\- A slowdown in consumer spending would hit retail and ad revenue hard. Discretionary shopping still drives a big chunk of Amazon's core engine.  
\- AWS growth has cooled, and further deceleration below 15% would pressure margins. Enterprise customers are still optimizing cloud spend, especially on AI workloads.  
\- International sellers, particularly from China, are cutting ad spend. That pullback risks slowing Amazon's high-margin advertising growth just as it's hitting scale.  
\- Regulatory pressure is mounting in the U.S. and EU. Antitrust cases targeting marketplace dominance or ad business practices could force structural changes.  
\- With so many bets in motion (AI, healthcare, Kuiper) execution risk is real. Missteps could slow momentum in a business built on speed.

Conclusion

Amazon isn't exactly falling apart. It's just hitting a stretch of road that requires tighter turns and sharper reflexes. The long-term thesis is still rock solid. This is the most integrated, diversified, and strategically entrenched tech platform on the planet. But the margin pressure from tariffs, a cooler consumer, and the cost of staying on top in AI and cloud? That's real, and it's going to test how much near-term pain investors are willing to stomach.

Our $225 price target reflects the duality of Amazon right now: a powerhouse with deep moats and a packed innovation pipeline, but one that's also facing higher costs and lower valuation tolerance. This isn't a set-it-and-forget-it moment. It's a phase that demands patience, sharp eyes on margins, and confidence that Amazon can keep doing what it does best: outbuilding everyone else.

6\. Coeur Mining, Inc. (CDE)  
Current Price: $20.95 | Price Target: $34 (36% upside) | Rating: Overweight | Upcoming Earnings: May 6  
Article Title: "Why Coeur Mining Is Moby's Stock Pick of the Year for 2026"

Moby's Stock Pick of the Year for 2026 is Coeur Mining, and it earns that title for a simple reason: the company fixed its problems just as the world started needing what it produces. After years of heavy spending, Coeur has crossed the toughest line in mining, shifting from building mines to actually generating cash. That shift is happening at the same time silver and gold are becoming harder to source and more important to the global economy. Rochester is now a large, reliable U.S. silver mine, Las Chispas delivers steady profits, and North American metal supply is no longer a nice bonus but a real priority. The market still treats Coeur like a short-term metals trade, but it increasingly looks like a core materials business. A $34 price target by Q4 2026 reflects that change. Execution still matters, because mining always finds a way to test patience, but heading into 2026, the setup is unusually straightforward.

Why Coeur Is Winning

Coeur's story only really makes sense if you zoom out for a moment.

Coeur has been mining precious metals since 1928, which makes it older than the S\&P 500 and older than most modern investing frameworks that try to explain it. Over nearly a century, the company has lived through every version of the mining business. It survived the Great Depression, the gold standard, the end of the gold standard, inflationary spikes, commodity supercycles, and long stretches where precious metals barely registered with investors. For much of that time, Coeur behaved exactly like a miner with that history. Cyclical, capital intensive, occasionally exciting, and often frustrating.

There were long periods where the company spent too much at the wrong point in the cycle and leaned too heavily on capital markets to stay afloat. Those years punished shareholders, but they also forced change. Assets were sold. Balance sheets were repaired. Discipline replaced optimism. Over time, Coeur built a portfolio designed to survive downturns rather than chase headlines during booms.

That long, uneven history matters now because the timing finally flipped.

Gold and silver are moving higher for reasons that do not require a commodities degree to understand. Start with gold. Governments are running persistent deficits, central banks expanded money supply aggressively over the past decade, and geopolitical tension has become a steady background noise rather than a temporary shock. When trust in currencies, debt, and political stability starts to fray, gold tends to reassert itself. Central banks are buying it. Investors are holding it. Prices are responding.

Silver adds another layer, and it is where Coeur's relevance sharpens.

Silver is something the modern world uses constantly. Solar panels depend on it. Electric vehicles rely on it. Power grids, data centers, and high-speed networks need it to function properly. Silver is one of the best conductors of electricity on the planet, and there is no substitute that performs as well. You can try to use a little less, but you cannot remove it without compromising performance.

Demand for silver has been rising steadily as solar capacity expands and electrification accelerates. Supply has not kept up. New mines take years to permit and build, and many of the world's silver-producing regions are becoming more difficult to operate in. That mismatch has been building quietly for a long time. Recent price moves reflect the market starting to acknowledge it.

This is where Coeur fits naturally.

The Rochester mine in Nevada is now one of the largest domestic silver operations in the United States. That detail carried less weight a decade ago. Today it matters. When supply chains were smooth and global, buyers focused on price alone. Reliability barely entered the conversation. Now it does. Domestic production reduces risk, shortens logistics, and removes political uncertainty from sourcing decisions. Rochester gives Coeur scale and location at the same time.

Las Chispas adds stability. Acquired through SilverCrest, it delivers high grades and strong margins that smooth results across price cycles. It produces consistent cash rather than drama. That consistency allows Coeur to push volume at Rochester without reopening old balance sheet wounds. In mining, dependable assets change behavior. They allow patience.

Gold production rounds out the picture. It balances revenue, dampens volatility, and provides protection when global uncertainty rises. Higher gold prices strengthen that contribution, but Coeur just needs it to keep doing what gold has done for centuries during uncertain periods.

Geography ties everything together. Coeur operates primarily in the United States, Canada, and Mexico. These are places where mines get permitted, contracts are enforced, and rules change slowly. In an industry where jurisdictional risk can erase value overnight, that stability carries real weight. It reduces surprises and lowers long-term risk.

Put together, Coeur looks less like a speculative metals story and more like a company that spent decades preparing for a moment it did not control. It fixed its balance sheet, built real assets, and waited. Gold is rising as confidence in systems weakens. Silver is rising as electrification demand compounds. North American supply is becoming more valuable by the year.

After nearly a century of cycles, missteps, and rebuilds, Coeur finds itself holding assets the market suddenly can't live without.

Financials

After years of heavy spending, Coeur has crossed into the phase miners spend their lives trying to reach. The mines are built. The checks are coming in. By late 2025, the company was generating close to $2 million in free cash flow every day, with full-year free cash flow topping $550 million at current metal prices. EBITDA moved past $1 billion. Debt faded into the background. Cash balances climbed above $500 million.

That shift changes how the company operates. When cash is tight, every decision is defensive. When cash shows up consistently, choices open up. Coeur is now operating from that second position.

You can see it in earnings. The company returned to profitability in 2025, with earnings per share jumping more than 150% year over year. A trailing P/E in the mid-20s makes sense in that light. The market is still catching up to what "normal" earnings look like for this business.

Revenue growth over the past five years has been steady rather than explosive, running in the high single digits to mid-teens. That turns out to be a feature, not a flaw. Coeur avoided the kind of hype cycle that leaves no room for upside. The current earnings lift is coming from mines maturing and costs settling down, not from unrealistic growth assumptions.

Opportunities

The upside for Coeur goes well beyond where gold or silver trade next month.

One opportunity is simple and increasingly valuable: location. As silver becomes more important to energy infrastructure, reliable North American supply matters more. Rochester's size and location give Coeur an advantage that few peers can replicate quickly.

Another is scale. The proposed New Gold acquisition would increase production, extend mine life, and add cash flow, all within the same regional footprint. There are approvals to clear and integration work to do, but the logic follows the same discipline Coeur has shown recently. Growth funded by cash, instead of stretching the balance sheet.

Then there is capital allocation. With most of the debt behind it, Coeur has options. Buybacks, dividends, internal investments, or selective deals all become possible without running back to the market. That flexibility is rare in mining and often overlooked because it shows up quietly rather than in splashy announcements.

There is also longer-term upside embedded in projects like Silvertip. It offers exposure to future silver demand without forcing Coeur to spend heavily today. The optionality is real, and it is funded by existing operations rather than hope.

Stock Outlook

The stock still trades like investors are waiting for the other shoe to drop. That hesitation is the opportunity.

On asset-based measures, the market already gives Coeur credit for a better portfolio and safer jurisdictions. On cash flow, the stock still reflects skepticism around execution. Reality likely lands between those views, and that gap is where reratings come from.

Rising gold and silver prices help, but they are not doing all the work. Even without the recent surge, Coeur would still look better positioned than it has in years. Stronger prices simply speed things up.

Mining always comes with caveats, and Coeur is no exception.

Metal prices still matter. Even with supportive long-term trends, short-term swings can pressure valuations.

Conclusion

Coeur Mining earns its place as Moby's Stock Pick of the Year for 2026 because it reflects what we want exposure to right now. Demand for silver tied to the energy transition is real. Secure North American supply is increasingly valuable. And Coeur has already done the difficult work of fixing its business before the market demanded it.

The stock still sits in an in-between phase, no longer distressed but not fully trusted either. If execution holds and metals prices remain supportive, that gap closes. When it does, the next leg of the story is driven less by hope and more by cash.

By the time that shift feels obvious, most of the opportunity will already be behind it.
